Exploratory Analysis of 2016 U.S. Congressional Candidates

Lab 4: Tableau Data Visualization

Overview / Lab Requirements

For this lab, I worked with a dataset focused on the 2016 presidential candidates and built a Tableau dashboard to visualize political patterns across states. I had to troubleshoot the dataset setup, choose visual formats that made sense for political data, and reflect on what the dashboard showed and what Tableau still struggled to show clearly.

  • Download and prepare a usable CSV dataset.
  • Build a dashboard using map and chart views to show candidate patterns.
  • Write a reflection on process, findings, and Tableau limitations.

What I Learned

Through this lab, I learned that data is not perfect and it is not always labeled in the same way, so you have to think critically about what the fields mean before you can even start building visuals. I also learned that design choices matter a lot because the chart types you choose affect how clearly people can understand political patterns in the data.

What This Demonstrates About My Abilities

This demonstrates that I can troubleshoot technical issues, work through messy data, and make design decisions based on the kind of story I want the data to tell. It also shows that I can use Tableau to build a clean dashboard, interpret patterns, and evaluate where the tool is strong and where deeper statistical analysis is still needed.

Reflection

Process and Troubleshooting

The first problem I had while trying to complete this lab was the CSV file not downloading properly. When looking through the different dataset options, I was drawn to one that focused on the data around the 2016 presidential candidates. I found my dataset quickly, but when I opened it, it just kept giving me raw text. To figure out how to fix this, I asked ChatGPT and learned how to manually save it. The next problem I ran into was Tableau not labeling "Number of Records" clearly, which forced me to look up what the other name for it could be in order to know what section to put it under and how to use it properly. There were also unknown locations on the map, which confused me at first. Through these issues, I learned that data is not perfect and it is not all the same, so you have to think critically about the different names involved in a dataset.

Design Decisions

When choosing the design I wanted to use to visualize my data, I had to think about what would best reflect political data. For this dataset, I thought maps would be helpful to see the distribution of candidates and their parties. I also thought having circle sizes would be helpful so you can quickly glance and see where the most candidates from each party were from. I also thought a bar chart would add more context because it would be another easy way to see the distinctions between the parties and the states.

Findings and Interpretation

Prior to visualizing this dataset, I would have assumed that larger states like California, Texas, and Florida would have more candidates, and I believe the visualization did confirm that. I also would have expected that the circles in the South would lean more Republican and the coastal states would lean more Democratic. California had the largest cluster, but something that surprised me was how thin the independent and other category was. This made me curious about why certain states attract more independent candidates and if candidate volume correlates with population size. Personally, I think it is a fair assumption that there would be a correlation between population and candidate volume because it is logical that the more people there are, the more would be willing to run. It was interesting to see how the amount of candidates spread out across states, and I would like to look into how each state's voting reacted to the different candidates.

Tool Assessment

I think Tableau helped a lot because it had very easy geographic visualization, and it was cool how you can drag things around and play with the layout until you are able to see the data in a way that makes sense. I also thought it was able to make a clean dashboard, which was helpful as a final way to see the different ways I was able to interpret the data. Tableau struggled a little with the statistical depth that may be needed to fully understand this dataset, and it was a little hard at times to compare the maps and the scales across all the sheets, but I still think it was a good start for visualizing this data.
