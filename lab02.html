AI Tool Evaluation

Lab 2: Analyzing AI Tools in Digital Culture

Back to Portfolio Home

Overview / Lab Requirements

For this lab, I tested ChatGPT and Canva Magic Studio to see how useful they actually are for school and creative work.

  • Figure out what each tool does well and where it falls short.
  • Talk through when each tool is appropriate and where ethics come in.
  • Back up the evaluation with examples from my own testing.

What I Learned

I learned that AI works best for me as a starting point, not a final answer. ChatGPT helped me brainstorm and organize ideas, and Canva sped up visual layout, but I still had to revise everything to make it specific, accurate, and actually sound like me.

What This Demonstrates About My Abilities

This lab shows that I can use digital tools critically instead of just accepting whatever they generate. It demonstrates that I can compare tools across writing and design tasks, call out ethical concerns, and explain where human decisions still matter most.

Reflection

ChatGPT

Capabilities: For me, ChatGPT worked best for brainstorming and organizing thoughts. It helped me outline ideas, reword rough sentences, and break prompts into smaller pieces so writing felt less overwhelming. The downside is that vague prompts usually give generic responses, and it still does not know class-specific context unless I provide it. I had to revise a lot to keep things accurate and original.

Appropriate Use: I think ChatGPT is best in the early stages, like idea generation and organizing a draft. It is not a good replacement for final writing, original arguments, or source-based analysis without human review. If I rely on it too heavily, the work can sound flat and less personal.

Ethical Considerations: The biggest ethical issue is losing original voice and ownership of the work. ChatGPT can help with outlines and ideas, but it is still on me to think critically, rewrite the content, and make sure the final result reflects my own understanding.

Canva Magic Studio

Capabilities: Canva Magic Studio was great for quick visual structure and layout ideas. It made clean-looking slide designs fast, which helped when I needed to move quickly. The weak spot was the text, which was usually too vague and needed editing. So even when the design looked polished, I still had to rewrite content for clarity and accuracy.

Appropriate Use: Canva works best for first drafts, visual inspiration, and speeding up formatting. It is less reliable for final presentations where messaging, data accuracy, and custom branding really matter. If I overuse it, the result can look nice but feel generic.

Ethical Considerations: The main concern here is originality. Since lots of users are drawing from similar AI suggestions, designs can start to look repetitive. I still need to customize and make sure the final product reflects my own thinking and work.

Broader Reflections

My main takeaway is that AI tools are best for brainstorming and first drafts, not final submissions. ChatGPT helped me sort ideas, and Canva helped me turn ideas into visuals, but neither replaces my own judgment or creativity. In the DCDA program, these tools are useful for experimenting and saving time, but using them too much can weaken skills like critical thinking and original design. The human part is still the context, strategy, and decision-making.
